Poshmark Design Systems Typography

One type system, every surface

Poshmark's typography was a patchwork of local decisions accumulated over years. This is how we turned inconsistency into a unified design standard — spanning iOS and Android — in six weeks.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Timeline
Typography System · 6 weeks
Tools
Figma
Scope
iOS · Android
Background

What the absence of a system costs

At Poshmark's scale, with the app spanning iOS and Android, small typographic inconsistencies don't stay small. Every time a designer made a local size or weight decision, that decision compounded across hundreds of components, screens, and teams — creating a product that felt subtly misaligned at every seam.

There was no shared typographic language. Font sizes, weights, and styles were chosen by convention and instinct, not by system. The same component type — a section label, a card title, a filter chip — could render at three different sizes depending on which designer or which quarter it shipped. Developers couldn't trust which spec was authoritative. Design reviews burned time on typographic debates that should have been settled by a reference.

This wasn't a style problem. It was a product velocity problem. And the cost was invisible until you looked at the whole surface area at once.

The Problem
iOS and Android had each evolved independently. There was no shared logic, no shared language — just years of local decisions with no referee.
Why a system, not a style guide

A static style guide would document what exists. A system defines what should be and why — with semantic roles, platform-specific implementation notes, and usage examples that make the right choice easier than the wrong one. We needed something designers would actually use, not just reference. That meant the output had to be opinionated, contextual, and embedded directly in Figma where the work happens.

Process

Four phases, one system

The work ran from December 2024 through January 2025. Rather than designing in isolation and presenting a finished system, we structured the process to build shared ownership — which mattered as much as the output.

Phase 01
Production Audit

I pulled live screens across iOS and Android and documented the existing typographic state. The audit surfaced multiple operating scales with no shared semantic logic — filter labels alone ranged from 11px to 14px across surfaces. The audit created a shared factual baseline: before we could agree on what should be, we needed to agree on what was.

Phase 02
Parallel Exploration

Rather than designing the system myself and asking for feedback, I brought three designers in and gave each a structured prompt to interpret a shared scale in their own product context. The Playground sessions ran independently, then converged. This surfaced the real constraints: what felt right in the feed didn't work in account settings; the right body size for listing cards differed from notification text.

Phase 03
Comparative Testing

We applied candidate systems to actual production screens — search results, product detail pages, account settings, and social feeds. Testing against real content (not lorem ipsum) exposed issues that spec sheets couldn't: sizes that felt comfortable in one context created visual noise in another. The comparative screens became the evidence base for every key decision we made.

Phase 04
Documentation

The final system was documented as a structured Figma file — not a flat table of specs, but an annotated, navigable reference covering usage examples, platform-specific notes, do/don't guidance, and the rationale behind key decisions. The goal was a document that any designer could open and apply without needing context from me. It shipped in January 2025 as the single source of truth for iOS and Android.

Stakeholder Alignment

Before the audit began, I aligned with the Head of Design and the iOS and Android engineering leads on scope, timeline, and what adoption would require from each team. Rather than presenting a finished system for sign-off, I ran two lightweight check-ins mid-process — one after the audit to share the baseline findings, one before documentation to confirm the key decisions before they were codified. By the time the system shipped, every stakeholder had seen the evidence, understood the rationale, and had a voice in the output. Adoption wasn't a change management challenge — it was already solved before launch.

Key Decisions

Where the system earned its constraints

Four choices defined the system. Each had genuine disagreement, real tradeoffs, and evidence behind it — not just aesthetic preference. These are the moments where a design system either earns its authority or becomes a document people route around.

01
Unified semantic scale vs. platform-native specs

The first question was structural: should iOS and Android get separate type scales to honour native conventions, or should we define one semantic scale with platform-specific rendering notes? Separate scales would be more platform-idiomatic but would double the maintenance burden and create the exact cross-platform drift we were trying to eliminate. We chose a shared semantic scale — naming roles rather than sizes (Body, Label, Caption, Title) — with platform-specific implementation notes embedded in each token. One source of truth; two rendering contexts.

Semantic naming across iOS and Android. Platform rendering notes live beside each style definition — not as a separate file.
02
Codifying 13px all-caps as the utility label standard

Small-cap labels — filter names, section dividers, metadata chips, form field labels — appeared throughout the product with inconsistent treatment. Some were 11px, some 13px; some all-caps, some title case; letter-spacing ranged from none to 2px. The comparative testing phase made the answer clear: 13px / 700 weight / 1.5px letter-spacing / all-caps rendered legibly at small sizes, passed accessibility contrast in both light and dark contexts, and felt consistent with Poshmark's brand register. We codified it as the utility label standard across all surfaces.

13px / Bold / All-caps / 1.5px letter-spacing defined as the canonical utility label. Applied to filters, metadata, section headers, and form labels across all surfaces.

Before/after showing the same filter labels and section dividers at inconsistent sizes vs. the standardised 13px all-caps treatment.
03
Body size: 14px vs 16px on mobile

The body size debate was the most contentious. The Playground phase surfaced strong preferences: some designers felt 16px was too large for content-dense mobile lists; others felt 14px was too small for comfortable reading on smaller devices. The comparative testing resolved it — applied to real product screens including feed cards, search results, and account settings, 14px held up for compact data-heavy contexts, while 16px was clearly better for paragraph-style body copy like descriptions and listing details. We landed on 14px as the default app body with 16px reserved for reading-intensive surfaces.

14px default app body / 16px for reading-heavy surfaces. Context-driven, not arbitrary.
04
Naming roles, not sizes

Early drafts of the system named styles by their pixel value: "12px Medium", "16px Regular", "20px Bold". This was intuitive for the first design, but broke down the moment a size needed to change for platform, context, or accessibility. A designer using "16px Regular" was making a size decision. A designer using "Body / Regular" was making a role decision — and the system could handle the size. We refactored every style name to reflect semantic role: Display, Title, Heading, Body, Label, Caption, Micro. Sizes became implementation details, not identifiers.

Semantic style naming throughout: Display · Title · Heading · Body · Label · Caption · Micro. Sizes are attributes of roles, not identifiers for designers to hunt.

The Output

A Figma file that does the thinking

The final deliverable was a structured Figma document serving as the single typographic authority for the entire product team. It wasn't built to be read once and archived — it was built to be the reference that ends typographic debates in real time. Every style includes the size, weight, line-height, letter-spacing, platform note, usage context, and an annotated example from a live product screen.

Impact

What a single source of truth changes

The system shipped in January 2025. The immediate impact wasn't dramatic — good design systems rarely announce themselves. The value showed up in the absence of decisions: fewer typographic debates in design review, fewer QA flags for spec inconsistencies, fewer back-and-forth Slack threads about "what size is a filter label again."

For Designers
Fewer decisions, faster work

Every new feature started with a resolved typographic foundation. Designers applied semantic roles rather than making size decisions from scratch. The system absorbed the low-level work, freeing design attention for higher-order problems — layout, hierarchy, user flow — rather than "is this 14px or 15px?"

For Engineering
One authoritative spec per surface

Before the system, engineers making implementation decisions had to interpret ambiguous specs or pull from multiple sources across different Figma files. The typography system gave them a single, platform-specific reference — reducing interpretation overhead and the type of specification drift that compounds into product debt over time.

For the Product
A more cohesive visual identity

Typography is the most repeated element in any digital product. Consistency at that layer compounds across every screen, every feature, every surface. The visual identity became more coherent not through a rebrand, but through the elimination of hundreds of micro-inconsistencies that had accumulated over years.

For the Team
Shared language, shared ownership

Because the system was built through collaborative exploration rather than handed down from a single designer, the team had genuine ownership of the output. The Playground sessions created buy-in before the final decisions were made — which is why the system was adopted without resistance and evolved iteratively as edge cases surfaced.

Reflection

What this taught me about systems thinking

On process
The Playground was the most valuable phase

Letting three designers interpret the problem independently before converging on a solution meant the final system had buy-in, not just sign-off. People don't resist systems they helped build. If I had designed the system myself and presented it for feedback, I'd have gotten polite alignment on something people didn't feel ownership over. The parallel exploration made the difference.

On constraint
The hardest part was agreeing on what to constrain

A typography system is, by definition, an exercise in agreed-upon constraint. The technical decisions — sizes, weights, scales — were straightforward once we had the data. The human decisions — getting three designers to agree that a shared constraint was worth more than individual flexibility — took longer and required more facilitation than the design work itself. That's the part design school doesn't teach.

On naming
Semantic names are a design decision, not a label

Moving from pixel-based names to role-based names changed how designers thought about typography in a feature. "Body" signals intent; "16px" signals measurement. A system that names roles teaches designers to think in roles — which means the system shapes behaviour, not just output. That's the kind of leverage that makes design systems worth building.

On scope
Start with the surface that has the most entropy

We started the audit with the screen type that had the widest variance — feed and search, where component density meant typographic decisions compounded fastest. That was the right call. Fixing the highest-entropy surface first created the clearest contrast between before and after, which generated team momentum for the rest of the system work. On the next initiative, I'd start the same way.

"A typography system isn't the sum of its sizes. It's the decisions that make those sizes defensible — and the process that makes the team trust them."
