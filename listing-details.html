Poshmark Commerce A/B Testing

17 million views, one chance to convert

Poshmark's listing details page was the most visited page on the platform and the most cluttered. This is how a phased redesign turned it into a focused, conversion-driven experience.

Role
Manager, Product Design
Timeline
Listing Details · 4 phases
Platforms
iOS · Android · Web
Outcome
Graduated to 100% of users
My Role

I led this initiative as Design Manager, owning problem framing, stakeholder alignment, and the decision to structure the work as four independent phases rather than a single redesign. I set the strategic direction and hypothesis for each phase, managed the cross-functional coordination across iOS, Android, and Web, and made the graduation call on Phase 1 during active stakeholder disagreement about the First Match drop. Two designers on my team owned the screen-level execution — one focused on mobile, one on web — and I embedded with them weekly to shape design direction and unblock tradeoffs. This case study reflects the decisions I owned, the tradeoffs I navigated, and the outcomes I was accountable for.

Context

The most important page we hadn't redesigned

The listing details page is where every purchase decision on Poshmark happens. A buyer arrives, sees the item, reads the details, and decides to buy, offer, or leave. At 17 million daily views, even marginal improvements in how that decision gets made compound into significant business outcomes.

The problem was that the page had been built incrementally. Each team added sections over time without a unified information architecture. The result was a page that mixed trust signals with noise, buried the purchase actions behind exploratory links, and presented information without visual priority. It wasn't a bad page. It was a page that had never been designed as a whole.

The Problem
The page wasn't designed to help buyers decide. It was designed to hold everything, and over time, everything had accumulated.
What the page was doing
  • Category, style tag, and department links sent buyers away from the listing before they could form purchase intent
  • Buy Now and Offer buttons competed with exploratory navigation, requiring buyers to scroll past low-intent paths to reach a decision
  • Product details had no hierarchy, title, brand, price, size, and condition presented at the same visual weight
  • Images were displayed in a vertical scroll, requiring significant scrolling to see all photos on a high-trust, secondhand item
  • Promotional signals like New with Tags, Boutique, and shipping deals were buried in text rather than surfaced as trust anchors
What we needed instead
  • A page structured around the purchase decision, not around every possible action a buyer might take
  • Images that let buyers build trust quickly, with a horizontal gallery replacing the vertical scroll
  • A hierarchy that surfaces title, brand, price, and size first, the four things that determine purchase intent
  • Deals and trust signals elevated to a dedicated section with tappable context, not buried as metadata
  • Cross-merch exposure that increases naturally from better page structure, rather than through explicit links
The hypothesis

By restructuring the information architecture to reduce low-intent exits and surface purchase-critical details more clearly, we can increase conversion without cannibalising revenue or engagement. Every link we removed was a decision to trust the page hierarchy over the safety net of exploration.

Approach

Four phases, each independently tested

Rather than a single big-bang redesign, we broke the work into four independently shippable phases. Each phase had its own hypothesis, A/B test, and graduation decision before the next began. This let us move fast, build confidence with data, and de-risk the full overhaul across iOS, Android, and Web.

A key strategic call

I made a deliberate decision to run each phase against the existing listing details page, not a redesigned baseline.

I wanted to learn from real user behaviour on the page we actually had before touching the most critical commerce surface on the platform. Shipping improvements to the live page meant the data was grounded in how buyers actually behaved today — not how I imagined they'd behave in a future state I hadn't validated yet. It also meant every phase had to earn its place independently, without being carried by the halo of a full redesign.

Phase 0
Image Scroll

Buyers of secondhand goods rely heavily on photos to build trust. The original vertical layout required significant scrolling to see all images. We replaced it with a horizontal swipe gallery, giving buyers faster access to the full set of photos without losing context of the rest of the page. Shipped in 4 weeks.

Phase 1
Product Details Restructure

The core intervention. Removed all hyperlinks from product metadata, reordered the information hierarchy to surface title, brand, and price first, elevated deals and promotions to a dedicated row, standardised size display, and collapsed long descriptions. This was the highest-risk phase and the one with the clearest hypothesis.

Phase 2
CTA Placement Test

With the product details restructured, we tested whether Buy Now and Offer buttons should appear above or below the product details section. Two variants, A/B tested across logged-in users and visitors. The below-fold variant showed stronger directional performance, consistent with the hypothesis that buyers who read product details before seeing purchase options act with more intent. The result informed the default CTA position for the web rollout.

Phase 3
Web Rollout

Extended the app redesign to web, adapting the same hierarchy and link removal logic to the desktop and mobile web experience. Added visitor login retention so users returning after login landed back on the same listing, not the home page. Scoped to US and CA with v3 test infrastructure compatibility.

Phase 0 Deep Dive Image Scroll

We didn't assume horizontal was the answer. We tested four ways to show photos.

Secondhand buyers rely on photos more than almost any other signal — condition, authenticity, and fit are all visual judgments. The original vertical scroll worked but required significant effort. Before committing to horizontal, we ran a simultaneous A/B/C/D test across three challenger variants to find out which gallery pattern gave buyers the fastest path to confidence.

Variant A Control
Vertical Scroll

Original experience. Images stacked vertically, full-width, scrollable. Buyers saw each photo in sequence but had to scroll significantly to see all images before reaching product details.

Variant B Winner
Horizontal Swipe Gallery

Full-width images in a horizontal swipe carousel with a page indicator overlaid at the bottom. Buyers could see all photos without losing their position on the page. Product details stayed immediately below the fold.

Variant C
Split View Scroll

Full vertical image scroll pinned above a persistent product details panel. Buyers could scroll through photos while the price, title, and Buy Now button stayed visible below — allowing them to interact with both sections simultaneously without losing context.

Variant D
Split View with Top Nav

Image gallery in the top half of the screen with a persistent top navigation bar. Product details and purchase actions anchored below. The nav bar competed with image space, reducing the visible photo area at the most critical trust-building moment.

Why Variant B won

The horizontal swipe gallery gave buyers the fastest path to confidence without sacrificing access to the full photo set. Variant A's vertical scroll buried product details below multiple full-height images. Variant C's split view reduced image size to keep content visible, which hurt the trust-building quality of the photos. Variant D's tap gate added friction before buyers could even start comparing photos. Variant B let buyers move through images at their own pace while keeping product details immediately in reach below the fold.

Phase 1 Deep Dive Information Hierarchy

Information on this page is the key. But whose logic should it follow?

With the image scroll resolved, Phase 1 turned to the harder question: what order should the information on the page follow? The risk was defaulting to a hierarchy that made sense to the business — prominence by revenue contribution — rather than one that matched how buyers actually make decisions.

The problem with early research

Before getting my head down in Figma, I ran brainstorming sessions and gathered research on what the page should prioritise. Most of the outputs were leaning heavily toward the business angle — surface deals, promote high-margin categories, push cross-sell. Legitimate signals, but not the full picture.

What changed when I talked to real users

I went and spoke directly to buyers and sellers. The gap between what the business wanted to surface and what users were actually looking for to make a decision was significant. Users needed condition clarity, size confidence, and price context first — not promotions.

Card Sorting Exercise

I ran a card sorting exercise with both buyers and sellers to let the hierarchy emerge from behaviour rather than assumption. Participants were given cards representing every piece of information on the listing page and asked to arrange them in the order they would want to see it. The results consistently placed photos, title, size, condition, and price at the top — the trust signals — and pushed cross-sell links, seller stats, and promotional badges further down. The data gave us the confidence to reorder the page in a way that served user intent first, with business signals placed where they would be seen by buyers who had already decided to consider the item.

Getting the hierarchy right was the easy part

The card sorting exercise gave me a clear, user-validated information order. Getting the business to agree to test it was a different fight entirely.

Business owners were deeply skeptical. Their concern was that changing the order of information on the most critical commerce page in the product would cannibalize metrics — and if it did, it would not just kill Phase 1 but potentially stop the entire redesign project in its tracks. The resistance wasn't unreasonable. It was a high-stakes page and the proposed change was significant.

I negotiated. Instead of running the test across the full 36 user segments, I agreed to reduce the exposure to 14 segments. A smaller blast radius if it went wrong, enough signal to learn if it went right. We ran the test anyway.

The reordered information hierarchy tested cleanly across 14 segments. Surfacing photos, title, size, condition, and price as the top-of-page trust layer — ahead of cross-sell, promotional badges, and seller metadata — produced a measurable lift in conversion without cannibalising any of the metrics the business owners had flagged as at risk. The test gave us the data to graduate Phase 1 and carry the validated hierarchy into the full redesign.

Key Decisions

Where the design earned its tradeoffs

Four decisions defined Phase 1. Each had real disagreement, real tradeoffs, and data behind it. These are the moments where information architecture either improves conversion or creates new problems.

01
Remove all hyperlinks from product metadata

Category, subcategory, style tag, and department links had historically been clickable, sending buyers to browse pages. None of these pages had meaningful conversion rates. They served as exploratory exits from the highest-intent moment in the buyer journey. The debate was whether removing them would hurt discovery. The data said otherwise: Category and style tag pages had very low conversion rates, serving more as passive browsing paths than purchase paths. We removed clickability entirely and kept the metadata as non-interactive labels. The information stayed; the exit disappeared.

Category, subcategory, style tag, and color fields made non-clickable. Metadata remains visible; low-intent exits removed.
02
Move seller profile below photos, not above

The original page placed the seller profile bar at the top, immediately below the nav. This was a holdover from when seller trust was the primary purchase signal on a younger platform. Qual research and user interviews surfaced a clear behavioral shift: by this stage of the platform's maturity, buyers evaluated the item first and validated the seller second. Poshmark's seller ratings, social graph, and buyer protection had matured enough that the item itself had become the primary trust anchor. Moving the profile below photos, alongside social actions, let the item speak first. Title, brand, and price followed directly below, creating a clean top-to-bottom purchase evaluation flow. The seller profile became a trust reinforcement signal, not a navigation anchor.

Seller profile moved below photos. Item title, brand, and price now lead the page hierarchy.

03
Surface deals as active trust signals, not metadata

New with Tags, Boutique, Free Authentication, discounted shipping, and price drops had been presented as text metadata, easy to scan past. We redesigned them as a dedicated deals row with tappable buttons that pull up informational action sheets. Each button became a conversation starter, not just a label. The intent was to make trust signals feel earned rather than listed. A "Free Authentication" badge you can tap and read builds more confidence than the same two words in a text field.

Deals elevated to a tappable row with contextual action sheets for NWT, Boutique, Free Authentication, and shipping promotions.
04
Collapse descriptions at 500 characters

Long seller descriptions were one of the main causes of page length variance. Some listings had two-line descriptions; others had ten. This made the page feel inconsistent and pushed purchase CTAs further down for long-description listings. We introduced a 500-character threshold with a "Show more" link. The default state is clean. Buyers who want the full description can access it with one tap. Sellers' content is fully available; it just doesn't dominate the page for buyers who don't need it.

Description capped at 500 characters with a "Show more" link. Once expanded, remains expanded for the session until the buyer returns to the listing.

Results

The data said graduate

The redesign ran as an A/B test across 26% of app users on iOS and Android. After a two-week test window, the results were clear enough to recommend graduation. Overall business metrics were neutral to positive. Purchase intent metrics moved significantly. The recommendation was to ship.

Why First Match Dropped and Why We Shipped Anyway

First Match dropped -0.99%, driven by the No Purchase segment (-2.67%, representing 37% of all users). This triggered the hardest stakeholder conversation of the project. Mid-test, I was asked whether we should stop early. I held the graduation recommendation because the demand-match segmentation told a different story: the FM drop was a behaviour shift, not a revenue loss. Users were spending less time exploring the seller's closet from the listing page not because they were less engaged, but because they were making purchase decisions directly from the listing. D7 Order/FM conversion for the No Purchase segment improved +3.05%, and buyer orders in that segment increased +2.46%. I brought that segmentation into the stakeholder review rather than defending the aggregate number. The graduation recommendation was not unanimous, but it was grounded in the data. Two weeks after graduation, the metrics held.

Web Rollout

Extending the redesign to web

After graduating on mobile, we extended the redesign to web, adapting the same hierarchy and link removal logic to desktop and mobile web. The web rollout added two additional requirements: CTA placement testing above vs. below product details, and a visitor login retention fix that returned users to the same listing after signing in rather than the home page.

Web Changes
Same hierarchy, new surface constraints

The web rollout wasn't a straight port. Desktop's wider viewport changed how the image gallery, product details, and cross-merch grid stacked relative to each other. The hover-based interaction model on desktop also meant the deals row needed rethinking — tappable action sheets designed for touch didn't translate directly. We adapted the same information hierarchy while re-solving for the surface. Separate feature flags for logged-in users and visitors were required, including compatibility with the Cross Merch V3 visitor test running in parallel. Scoped to US and CA.

Visitor Login Fix
Return to listing after login

Visitors who signed in on a listing details page were previously redirected to the home page, breaking their purchase intent. The fix returned them to the exact listing they were viewing, maintaining context and reducing drop-off at the login conversion step.

Reflection

What this project taught me about conversion design

On the FM story
Model the expected shift before the test

The First Match drop created stakeholder anxiety mid-test that was difficult to manage in real time. If I had modelled the expected FM redistribution before launch, using demand match data we already had, we could have reframed the metric as a leading indicator of conversion shift rather than a loss. The story would have been the same. The conversation would have been easier.

On scope
Test description truncation independently

The 500-character collapse was bundled into Phase 1 rather than isolated in its own test. We shipped it with confidence that it was the right call, but we don't know its isolated impact on conversion or engagement for long-description listings. That's a decision that deserved its own data, and on the next initiative, it would get one.

On phased execution
Phasing is a stakeholder management tool, not just a risk tool

Four independent tests across 12 months meant four separate roadmap conversations, four alignment cycles, and four graduation decisions. The overhead was real. But it also meant every stakeholder had a clear entry point to question or support each change individually, without the weight of the whole redesign riding on one call. Phase 0's clean graduation gave us credibility for Phase 1's harder conversation. The phased structure didn't just de-risk the design — it de-risked the politics.

On cross-merch
Removing links increased cross-sell exposure

Cross-Merch Grid Views went up +4.01% despite removing explicit cross-merch navigation links from the product details section. The redesigned page created a more structured scroll path that surfaced the cross-merch grid to more buyers. This was a counterintuitive result that reinforced the core hypothesis: a focused page does more than a page that tries to do everything.

On growing the team
The designer who owned Phase 3 had never shipped a web surface before

One of the outcomes I'm most proud of from this project isn't in the metrics. The designer who owned the web rollout had previously focused exclusively on mobile. I pushed for her to lead Phase 3 because she had built enough context on the product logic through Phases 1 and 2 that the surface shift was the right kind of stretch. She navigated the cross-Merch V3 compatibility work, pushed back on an engineering scope cut that would have compromised the hierarchy, and shipped. That's the kind of ownership I want to build in every designer on my team — not just executing a spec, but holding the design intent under real constraints.

I came away from this project with a conviction that now shapes how I frame every conversion problem: the best commerce page isn't the one that offers the most paths. It's the one that makes the right path impossible to miss.
