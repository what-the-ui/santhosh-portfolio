Poshmark Commerce A/B Testing

17 million views, one chance to convert

Poshmark's listing details page was the most visited page on the platform and the most cluttered. This is how a phased redesign turned it into a focused, conversion-driven experience.

Role
Manager, Product Design
Timeline
Listing Details · 4 phases
Platforms
iOS · Android · Web
Outcome
Graduated to 100% of users
Context

The most important page we hadn't redesigned

The listing details page is where every purchase decision on Poshmark happens. A buyer arrives, sees the item, reads the details, and decides to buy, offer, or leave. At 17 million daily views, even marginal improvements in how that decision gets made compound into significant business outcomes.

The problem was that the page had been built incrementally. Each team added sections over time without a unified information architecture. The result was a page that mixed trust signals with noise, buried the purchase actions behind exploratory links, and presented information without visual priority. It wasn't a bad page. It was a page that had never been designed as a whole.

The Problem
The page wasn't designed to help buyers decide. It was designed to hold everything, and over time, everything had accumulated.
What the page was doing
  • Category, style tag, and department links sent buyers away from the listing before they could form purchase intent
  • Buy Now and Offer buttons competed with exploratory navigation, requiring buyers to scroll past low-intent paths to reach a decision
  • Product details had no hierarchy, title, brand, price, size, and condition presented at the same visual weight
  • Images were displayed in a vertical scroll, requiring significant scrolling to see all photos on a high-trust, secondhand item
  • Promotional signals like New with Tags, Boutique, and shipping deals were buried in text rather than surfaced as trust anchors
What we needed instead
  • A page structured around the purchase decision, not around every possible action a buyer might take
  • Images that let buyers build trust quickly, with a horizontal gallery replacing the vertical scroll
  • A hierarchy that surfaces title, brand, price, and size first, the four things that determine purchase intent
  • Deals and trust signals elevated to a dedicated section with tappable context, not buried as metadata
  • Cross-merch exposure that increases naturally from better page structure, rather than through explicit links
The hypothesis

By restructuring the information architecture to reduce low-intent exits and surface purchase-critical details more clearly, we can increase conversion without cannibalising revenue or engagement. Every link we removed was a decision to trust the page hierarchy over the safety net of exploration.

Approach

Four phases, each independently tested

Rather than a single big-bang redesign, we broke the work into four independently shippable phases. Each phase had its own hypothesis, A/B test, and graduation decision before the next began. This let us move fast, build confidence with data, and de-risk the full overhaul across iOS, Android, and Web.

Phase 0
Image Scroll

Buyers of secondhand goods rely heavily on photos to build trust. The original vertical layout required significant scrolling to see all images. We replaced it with a horizontal swipe gallery, giving buyers faster access to the full set of photos without losing context of the rest of the page. Shipped in 4 weeks.

Phase 1
Product Details Restructure

The core intervention. Removed all hyperlinks from product metadata, reordered the information hierarchy to surface title, brand, and price first, elevated deals and promotions to a dedicated row, standardised size display, and collapsed long descriptions. This was the highest-risk phase and the one with the clearest hypothesis.

Phase 2
CTA Placement Test

With the product details restructured, we tested whether Buy Now and Offer buttons should appear above or below the product details section. Two variants, A/B tested across both logged-in users and visitors, with separate feature flags for the Cross Merch V3 web test running in parallel.

Phase 3
Web Rollout

Extended the app redesign to web, adapting the same hierarchy and link removal logic to the desktop and mobile web experience. Added visitor login retention so users returning after login landed back on the same listing, not the home page. Scoped to US and CA with v3 test infrastructure compatibility.

Key Decisions

Where the design earned its tradeoffs

Four decisions defined Phase 1. Each had real disagreement, real tradeoffs, and data behind it. These are the moments where information architecture either improves conversion or creates new problems.

01
Remove all hyperlinks from product metadata

Category, subcategory, style tag, and department links had historically been clickable, sending buyers to browse pages. None of these pages had meaningful conversion rates. They served as exploratory exits from the highest-intent moment in the buyer journey. The debate was whether removing them would hurt discovery. The data said otherwise: Category and style tag pages had very low conversion rates, serving more as passive browsing paths than purchase paths. We removed clickability entirely and kept the metadata as non-interactive labels. The information stayed; the exit disappeared.

Category, subcategory, style tag, and color fields made non-clickable. Metadata remains visible; low-intent exits removed.
02
Move seller profile below photos, not above

The original page placed the seller profile bar at the top, immediately below the nav. This was a holdover from when seller trust was the primary purchase signal. User research indicated that buyers now evaluate the item first and the seller second. Moving the profile below photos, alongside social actions, let the item speak first. Title, brand, and price followed directly below, creating a clean top-to-bottom purchase evaluation flow. The seller profile became a trust reinforcement signal, not a navigation anchor.

Seller profile moved below photos. Item title, brand, and price now lead the page hierarchy.

Side-by-side of the original page hierarchy vs. the redesigned hierarchy, annotated to show the seller profile repositioning and new title/brand/price ordering.
03
Surface deals as active trust signals, not metadata

New with Tags, Boutique, Free Authentication, discounted shipping, and price drops had been presented as text metadata, easy to scan past. We redesigned them as a dedicated deals row with tappable buttons that pull up informational action sheets. Each button became a conversation starter, not just a label. The intent was to make trust signals feel earned rather than listed. A "Free Authentication" badge you can tap and read builds more confidence than the same two words in a text field.

Deals elevated to a tappable row with contextual action sheets for NWT, Boutique, Free Authentication, and shipping promotions.
04
Collapse descriptions at 500 characters

Long seller descriptions were one of the main causes of page length variance. Some listings had two-line descriptions; others had ten. This made the page feel inconsistent and pushed purchase CTAs further down for long-description listings. We introduced a 500-character threshold with a "Show more" link. The default state is clean. Buyers who want the full description can access it with one tap. Sellers' content is fully available; it just doesn't dominate the page for buyers who don't need it.

Description capped at 500 characters with a "Show more" link. Once expanded, remains expanded for the session until the buyer returns to the listing.

Results

The data said graduate

The redesign ran as an A/B test across 26% of app users on iOS and Android. After a two-week test window, the results were clear enough to recommend graduation. Overall business metrics were neutral to positive. Purchase intent metrics moved significantly. The recommendation was to ship.

Why First Match Dropped and Why We Shipped Anyway

First Match dropped -0.99%, driven by the No Purchase segment (-2.67%, representing 37% of all users). This was the key stakeholder debate. The data told a nuanced story: the FM drop was a behaviour shift, not a revenue loss. Users were spending less time exploring the seller's closet from the listing page, not because they were less engaged, but because they were making purchase decisions directly from the listing. D7 Order/FM conversion for the No Purchase segment improved +3.05%, and overall buyer orders in that segment increased +2.46%. Users clicking less on the seller's name from the listing page contributed to 70% of the community closet page view loss, but the orders followed them to the listing. The redesign was working as intended.

Web Rollout

Extending the redesign to web

After graduating on mobile, we extended the redesign to web, adapting the same hierarchy and link removal logic to desktop and mobile web. The web rollout added two additional requirements: CTA placement testing above vs. below product details, and a visitor login retention fix that returned users to the same listing after signing in rather than the home page.

Web Changes
Same hierarchy, new surface constraints

The web experience required separate feature flags for logged-in users and visitors, including compatibility with the Cross Merch V3 visitor test running in parallel. Two CTA placement variants were tested independently. Scoped to US and CA.

Visitor Login Fix
Return to listing after login

Visitors who signed in on a listing details page were previously redirected to the home page, breaking their purchase intent. The fix returned them to the exact listing they were viewing, maintaining context and reducing drop-off at the login conversion step.

Reflection

What this project taught me about conversion design

On the FM story
Model the expected shift before the test

The First Match drop created stakeholder anxiety mid-test that was difficult to manage in real time. If I had modelled the expected FM redistribution before launch, using demand match data we already had, we could have reframed the metric as a leading indicator of conversion shift rather than a loss. The story would have been the same. The conversation would have been easier.

On scope
Test description truncation independently

The 500-character collapse was bundled into Phase 1 rather than isolated in its own test. We shipped it with confidence that it was the right call, but we don't know its isolated impact on conversion or engagement for long-description listings. That's a decision that deserved its own data, and on the next initiative, it would get one.

On phased execution
The phased approach was worth its overhead

Running four independent tests across roughly 12 months added coordination overhead that a single redesign wouldn't have. But it also meant each decision was backed by its own data, each phase built confidence for the next, and the graduation decision at the end was made with evidence from every part of the change. The overhead was the cost of being able to answer "why" for every choice.

On cross-merch
Removing links increased cross-sell exposure

Cross-Merch Grid Views went up +4.01% despite removing explicit cross-merch navigation links from the product details section. The redesigned page created a more structured scroll path that surfaced the cross-merch grid to more buyers. This was a counterintuitive result that reinforced the core hypothesis: a focused page does more than a page that tries to do everything.

"The best commerce page isn't the one that offers the most paths. It's the one that makes the right path impossible to miss."
